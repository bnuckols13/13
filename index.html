Today is Friday the thirteenth. A day the world has agreed to call unlucky. Cursed. Dangerous. But consider this: the number thirteen was sacred long before it was feared. Thirteen moons in a year. Thirteen weeks in each season. Thirteen — the number of transformation, of cycles completing, of something new beginning.

And Friday — named for Freya, Norse goddess of love, beauty, and fierce protection. A day that once belonged to the feminine, to pleasure, to power. It was made unlucky by those who feared that power.

So today, we ask: What in your life has been called unlucky, broken, too much, or not enough — that might actually carry a secret strength? What part of you was cast out that deserves to come home?

Perhaps it is your sensitivity. Your intensity. Your need for rest. Your body. Your appetite. Your grief. Your strangeness. Your particular way of being alive.

Today we flip the script. We take the cursed and call it blessed. We take the rejected and crown it. We take the number thirteen and make it our lucky number.

When you are ready, let your eyes gently open. We begin.

☼ Today's Invitation

We live inside stories that other people wrote about us. Stories about what makes us broken, what makes us unlucky, what we should fear or hide. Today, we pick up the pen. We rewrite the superstition. We flip the curse into a blessing and discover that what was cast out was powerful all along.

The Therapeutic Frame: This group draws on narrative therapy (reauthoring dominant stories), IFS (welcoming exiled parts home), and post-traumatic growth (meaning-making after adversity). When we reclaim what was labeled “cursed,” we reclaim agency over our own story.

★ The Lore of the “Unlucky”

Every superstition holds a hidden history. What we call “cursed” was often sacred first. Tap each card to explore.

1 The Number 13 Was Sacred +

Thirteen lunar cycles make a year. There are thirteen weeks in each season. In many pre-Christian traditions, thirteen represented completion and transformation — the moment when a full cycle ends and something new begins.

The therapeutic mirror: What in your life feels like it’s completing a cycle? What transformation have others called “too much” or “too disruptive” that might actually be the natural rhythm of your becoming?

2 Friday Belonged to Freya +

Friday is named for Freya (Norse) or Frigga — goddesses of love, fertility, beauty, and fierce protection. Friday was a day of celebration and connection. It became “unlucky” as older traditions were suppressed and feminine power was demonized.

The therapeutic mirror: Where have you been told your softness is weakness? Where has your capacity for love, connection, or pleasure been pathologized or shamed?

3 Black Cats Were Revered +

In ancient Egypt, black cats were considered sacred protectors. In Celtic traditions, a black cat arriving at your home was a sign of prosperity. It was the European witch trials that transformed them into omens of evil — guilt by association with women who healed and knew too much.

The therapeutic mirror: What knowledge, intuition, or way of being have you been taught to distrust in yourself? What “darkness” in you might actually be quiet wisdom?

4 Walking Under Ladders & Broken Mirrors +

The ladder against a wall forms a triangle — a shape sacred to many traditions. To walk through it was to disturb the divine. Mirrors were thought to hold pieces of the soul; to break one was to fragment yourself.

The therapeutic mirror: What sacred boundaries have you been taught to fear instead of honor? And where in your life are you afraid of being “broken” — when perhaps you’re just in the process of being rearranged?

5 The Curse Is the Story — Not the Thing +

Narrative therapy teaches us that the person is not the problem; the problem is the problem. Superstitions work the same way — they attach fear to neutral or even beautiful things. The number, the cat, the mirror are not cursed. The story about them is.

The same is true for us. You are not broken. You are not cursed. You are not “too much.” Someone wrote a story about you that wasn’t true. Today, you get to rewrite it.

From IFS: The parts of us that carry shame were often our most creative, passionate, sensitive selves — exiled to protect us from rejection. Reclamation means inviting those exiled parts back with honor.

♦ Seven Stations of Reclamation

Choose any station that calls to you. Spend as long as you wish. There is no order, no requirement, no wrong way. You may always pass.

Tap a station to reveal its prompts.

△
Visual Arts
The Alchemy of Image
Curse to Blessing Portrait: On one side of the paper, represent something you’ve been told is “wrong” with you. On the other side, represent the hidden strength inside that same quality.
Lucky 13 Talisman: Design your own good luck charm, sigil, or amulet. What symbols represent your reclaimed power?
The Rewritten Label: Take a word that has been used against you (broken, lazy, too sensitive, dramatic, selfish). Transform it visually into something beautiful. Illuminate it like a medieval manuscript.
Before & After the Flip: Create a diptych — the “cursed” version of the story on the left, the reclaimed version on the right. Same material, different narrative.
Black Cat Portrait: Draw, paint, or collage the part of yourself that others fear or misunderstand. Give it dignity. Give it beauty.
∞
Movement & Dance
The Body Remembers What Words Forget
The Shrink and the Expand: Begin by making yourself as small as the “curse” told you to be. Slowly, expand into the fullness that was always yours. Take up space.
Walk of Reclamation: Walk across the room as the “cursed” version of yourself — head down, shoulders in. Then walk back as the reclaimed version. Notice what shifts in your body.
Shake It Off: Literally. Shake your hands, arms, legs, whole body. Animals discharge stress through shaking. Let the old stories leave through movement.
The Lucky Dance: If your reclaimed self had a victory dance, a strut, a celebratory movement — what would it look like? Let your body find it.
Mirror, Mirror: With a partner or alone, move as if you are seeing yourself clearly for the first time — not through the distorted mirror of superstition, but through the clear mirror of self-recognition.
♫
Music & Sound
Vibration as Reclamation
The Unlucky Playlist vs. The Lucky Playlist: Choose a song that represents the old “cursed” story. Then choose one that represents the flip. What changes?
Sound of the Curse Breaking: Using voice, instruments, or body percussion, create the sound of a curse shattering. What does liberation sound like?
Reclamation Anthem: If your reclaimed self had a theme song, a rallying cry, a battle hymn — what would it be? Hum it, sing it, drum it out.
Thirteen Beats: Create a rhythm pattern with exactly thirteen beats. Let it be irregular, unexpected, your own. Thirteen doesn’t fit neatly into 4/4 time, and neither do you.
Whisper to Roar: Start with the quietest sound you can make — the hushed voice of the exiled part. Slowly build to full volume. Let yourself be heard.
⌘
Writing
The Pen Rewrites the Curse
Letter to the Superstition: Write a letter to the belief, label, or story that cursed you. Tell it what it took from you. Then tell it what you’re taking back.
13 Things I Was Wrong About Myself: List thirteen things you believed about yourself that turned out to be someone else’s story, not yours.
The Counter-Spell: Write an incantation, poem, or declaration that reverses the curse. Begin with “I am not cursed. I am…”
Origin Story Rewrite: Choose a memory where you were shamed, rejected, or told you were too much. Rewrite it from the perspective of someone who sees your wholeness.
The Black Cat Speaks: Write from the perspective of a black cat who was sacred before it was feared. What would it say to the part of you that knows this feeling?
◇
Drama & Performance
Embodying the Flip
The Trial of the Cursed: Put the “curse” on trial. You are the defense attorney for the part of you that was labeled bad, broken, or unlucky. Make your closing argument.
Before & After Monologue: Perform a short monologue as the “cursed” version of yourself, then immediately perform one as the reclaimed version. Same person, different story.
The Coronation: Perform a ceremony where the exiled part of you is not just welcomed back — but crowned. What title does it receive? What honors?
Superstition Breaker: Physically and dramatically “break” a superstition. Walk under an imaginary ladder with triumph. Celebrate the number thirteen. Pet an imaginary black cat with reverence.
The Two Narrators: One voice tells the cursed version of your story. The other interrupts with the truth. Let them argue. See who wins.
✦
Fashion & Adornment
Wearing Your Reclamation
The Crown of the Reclaimed: Create a crown, headpiece, or adornment from available materials. This is for the part of you that was told it didn’t belong. Now it rules.
Lucky Charm Creation: Fashion a physical talisman — a lucky charm that represents your reclaimed narrative. Something you could carry with you.
Armor vs. Adornment: Create something you would wear that represents the difference between protecting yourself from the curse and adorning yourself in your own power.
The Thirteenth Outfit: If you were dressing for a celebration of everything the world told you to hide — what would you wear? Style it. Describe it. Embody it.
⁂
Open Intuitive
The Surprise Station
No Rules. Follow your impulse. Combine stations. Invent something new. If the muse is calling you somewhere unexpected — go there.
Cross-Pollinate: Take something you started at one station and finish it at another. Write a poem and then dance it. Draw your sound. Sing your painting.
The Thirteenth Thing: You have twelve prompts behind you. What is the thirteenth — the one nobody expected, the one that breaks the pattern? Let it find you.
You may always pass. You may sit quietly. You may watch. You may change stations at any time. Your nervous system knows what it needs.
